The Jobs to Be Done Framework: Understanding What Your Customer Actually Wants to Accomplish


Most sales conversations begin with a fundamental misunderstanding. The seller thinks the buyer wants *their product*, when really the buyer wants to accomplish something—and your product is just one possible tool among many. This is the insight behind Jobs to Be Done (JTBD), a framework developed by Clayton Christensen and refined by figures like Bob Moesta and Chris Spiens, that has quietly revolutionized how the best B2B salespeople think about their prospects.


The core idea is elegantly simple: people don't buy products or services; they hire them to do a job. When someone buys project management software, they're not hiring software—they're hiring the ability to coordinate a distributed team and maintain visibility over a chaotic project. When a company adopts a new CRM, they're hiring a system to help their sales team spend less time on administrative drudgery and more time actually selling. Understanding this distinction changes everything about how you sell.


What makes JTBD powerful in B2B selling is that it shifts focus away from feature conversations and toward meaning-making conversations. A traditional sales approach might highlight that your software has eight integrations, includes mobile apps, and provides real-time dashboards. A JTBD approach instead asks: *What were you struggling to do before you called us? What's currently frustrating about your workflow? What would success look like six months after implementing our solution?*


The framework distinguishes between functional, emotional, and social jobs. The functional job is the practical task—manage projects more efficiently. The emotional job is how the buyer wants to *feel*—confident, in control, not overwhelmed. The social job is how the buyer wants to *be perceived*—as organized, forward-thinking, a leader who uses modern tools. Top B2B sellers instinctively weave all three into their conversations because they understand that a prospect's decision to buy is rarely rational alone; it's wrapped in emotion and identity.


Consider how Salesforce's best account executives operate. They don't lead with product specs. Instead, they investigate the buyer's "struggle." Perhaps a VP of Sales is frustrated that her team submits reports manually every Friday, creating bottlenecks and errors. She's also embarrassed in front of the CEO that she can't instantly answer "How much pipeline do we have this quarter?" And she wants her team to see her as someone who's modernizing the department. A JTBD-oriented seller helps her name this entire constellation of jobs and then shows how the product *becomes* the means to accomplish them all—but the conversation stays focused on the jobs, not the software.


This framework also helps you understand competition differently. Your competitor isn't another CRM vendor; it's the status quo—the messy spreadsheets and email chains the buyer currently uses. Or it's the competing priority of fixing three other broken processes. By understanding all the jobs the buyer needs done, you can address the real hesitations, not the surface objections.


The practical benefit is that sales cycles become shorter when you're aligned with what the buyer actually values. Proposals are more compelling. Negotiations focus on outcomes rather than price. And most importantly, customers actually find success with what they buy because they bought a solution to a real, named job rather than a impressive list of features they didn't need.


💡 One thing to try today: On your next prospect call, ask: "What were you doing *before* you reached out to us—how were you handling [their challenge] yourself?" Listen for the struggle. Name it back to them. Only then mention your product.
