Agentic Workflows in Enterprise Software: When Your Tools Start Making Decisions


There's a quiet revolution happening in enterprise software that most traditional B2B vendors haven't quite reckoned with yet. It's not about artificial intelligence in the abstract sense—it's about the shift from tools that respond to human input toward systems that autonomously complete multi-step business processes without waiting for human approval at each stage. We're watching the emergence of what researchers at Anthropic and Replit are calling "agentic workflows," and they're fundamentally rewriting the contract between software and the people who use it.


For the past fifteen years, the B2B software industry has optimized around a single model: the user interface. A salesperson opens Salesforce, a marketer opens HubSpot, an engineer opens Jira. These tools present information and ask the user to decide what happens next. Click this button, fill that field, push that status forward. The entire industry—billions in market cap—was built on the assumption that humans remain in the decision loop. But agentic workflows are upending that assumption in a way that's both liberating and disorienting.


Consider what happens today when a customer success team needs to identify at-risk accounts. A CSM opens Gainsight or Totango, runs a report, analyzes the data, determines which accounts need intervention, schedules outreach, and logs activities. It's methodical and controlled—but it's also slow, and it scales only as fast as human attention permits. With agentic workflows, the same system observes patterns in customer behavior in real-time, autonomously triggers outreach sequences when risk signals appear, coordinates with sales about account transitions, and then reports back to the CSM with a summary of actions taken and outcomes achieved.


The distinction matters profoundly because it changes what software is optimizing for. Traditional B2B tools optimize for thoroughness and user control. Agentic systems optimize for velocity and outcome completeness. A Salesforce workflow might generate a task for a sales rep to follow up on a cold account. An agentic system might autonomously craft and send a contextually relevant message, monitor response patterns, and escalate to a human only if the interaction requires judgment that exceeds its training parameters.


Companies like Zapier, Make, and newer entrants like Modal are building infrastructure that makes this easier. But the real shift is deeper—it's happening inside the core products themselves. Notion's new AI features don't just suggest text; they're beginning to execute database operations. Monday.com's recent updates don't just alert users to bottlenecks; they're starting to auto-allocate resources and timeline work. Stripe is enabling systems that can autonomously retry failed payments with intelligent logic about timing and messaging.


The hesitation many enterprises feel is legitimate. Handing decision-making authority to software requires calibrating trust, understanding failure modes, and building in human override mechanisms. But the economic pressure is immense. Companies that keep humans in every decision loop will find themselves unable to compete with companies that deploy agentic workflows responsibly, completing work at machine speed while humans focus on judgment, creativity, and strategy.


The question isn't whether agentic workflows will reshape B2B software—they already are. The question is how quickly your industry will normalize autonomous decision-making by software, and whether your current tools can even be retrofitted to work that way.


💡 One thing to try today: Spend fifteen minutes mapping out one repetitive multi-step process in your work (customer onboarding, report generation, account triage—anything you do weekly). Write down each decision point where a human stops and evaluates before proceeding. That's your first candidate for agentic workflow redesign.
